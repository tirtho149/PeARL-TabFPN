\title{PEaRL+TabPFN: A Head-to-Head Comparison of MLP and Pretrained Tabular
Heads for Gene and Pathway Expression Prediction from Histology}

\author{\IEEEauthorblockN{Ushashi Bhattacharjee\textsuperscript{1}, Alloy Das\textsuperscript{1}, Saria Hannan\textsuperscript{1}, Tirtho Roy\textsuperscript{1}, and Soumik Sarkar}

Special Thanks: Koushik Howlader for valuable discussions and feedback}
}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
We present a head-to-head comparison between the original PEaRL framework
(Majumder et al., arXiv:2510.03455), which uses MLP heads to predict gene
and pathway expression from histology, and an otherwise-identical pipeline in
which the MLP heads are replaced 1:1 by a bank of TabPFN regressors
(\textit{PEaRL+TabPFN}). TabPFN is a pretrained transformer that performs
in-context tabular regression and has shown strong sample-efficiency on small
to medium tabular tasks; whether this advantage transfers to spatial
transcriptomics regression on top of a frozen pathology foundation model has
not been previously evaluated. To make the comparison rigorous we apply an
\textit{apple-to-apple} reproduction protocol: identical UNI v1 vision
backbone with the last four transformer blocks fine-tuned, identical
two-stage contrastive-then-supervised training, identical 8-neighbor
spatial smoothing, identical Reactome+MSigDB Hallmark pathway sources, and
identical 5-fold cross-validation on the HEST-1k breast cancer cohort. Only
the prediction head changes between conditions. We report PCC, MSE, and MAE
on both gene expression and pathway-activity prediction, plus per-dim PCC
distributions, spatial heatmaps, and pathway co-expression structure
diagnostics. Code, configs, and figures are released at
\url{https://github.com/tirtho149/PeARL-TabFPN}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
% ============================================================================

Predicting molecular profiles directly from H\&E whole-slide images is a
high-leverage problem in computational pathology: spatial transcriptomics
assays are expensive and slow, while histology images are routinely
acquired. The PEaRL framework~\cite{pearl2025} introduced a two-stage
contrastive-then-supervised approach in which (a) image and pathway
embeddings are aligned in a shared 256-dimensional space via NT-Xent loss
and (b) two MLP heads are then trained on the (frozen) image embeddings
to regress gene expression and pathway activation scores. On the HEST-1k
breast cancer cohort, PEaRL reports gene Pearson correlation
(PCC) of $0.587 \pm 0.036$ and pathway PCC of $0.506 \pm 0.027$,
outperforming prior baselines such as ST-Net, His2ST, BLEEP, and MCLSTExp.

A complementary line of work outside pathology has shown that
\textit{TabPFN}~\cite{tabpfn2023}, a transformer pretrained on
synthetically-generated tabular regression tasks, can match or exceed
gradient-boosted trees on small-to-medium tabular problems with a single
forward pass, no hyperparameter tuning, and only seconds of
``training.'' Since PEaRL's stage-2 head problem is, by construction, a
medium-sized tabular regression --- 256-dimensional fixed-encoder
embeddings as features, $\sim$10k spots as rows, and 1,000 + 775
output dimensions on Breast --- it is natural to ask whether TabPFN can
serve as a drop-in replacement for the MLP head.

This paper answers that question through a controlled head-to-head
comparison. Our contributions are:

\begin{itemize}
    \item An \textbf{apple-to-apple reproduction protocol} for PEaRL on
          HEST-1k Breast: 8-neighbor smoothing, Reactome$+$MSigDB Hallmark
          pathway pool, raw ssGSEA pathway scaling, last-4-block UNI
          fine-tuning, 5-fold CV with paper-matched
          (lr, weight decay, patience, epochs) settings, and
          per-fold metrics with constant-target columns filtered.
    \item A \textbf{1:1 head replacement} of PEaRL's two MLP heads by a
          bank of TabPFN regressors, one per output dimension. Both heads
          (MLP, TabPFN) see the same training data, the same stage-1
          contrastive pretraining, and the same frozen image embeddings.
    \item A \textbf{head-to-head evaluation} comprising PCC, MSE, MAE,
          per-dim PCC distributions, side-by-side spatial heatmaps, and
          pathway-pathway correlation matrices, with the PEaRL paper's
          reported numbers as a reference column throughout.
    \item Reproducible code and configs at
          \url{https://github.com/tirtho149/PeARL-TabFPN}, including a
          single \texttt{--apple-to-apple} CLI flag that bundles every
          paper-faithful setting.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
% ============================================================================

\textbf{Spatial gene expression prediction from histology.}
ST-Net~\cite{stnet}, His2ST~\cite{his2st}, BLEEP~\cite{bleep},
and MCLSTExp~\cite{mclstexp} all predict gene panels from histology
patches. MCLSTExp and BLEEP exploit contrastive learning between image and
expression embeddings; His2ST adds graph aggregation. PEaRL extends this
family by replacing per-gene targets with ssGSEA pathway scores during the
contrastive alignment, reducing dimensionality and yielding more
biologically coherent embeddings.

\textbf{Foundation models for pathology.}
UNI~\cite{uni2024} is a ViT-Large model trained via DINOv2 on
$\sim$100,000 H\&E WSIs. It is the strongest commonly-available frozen
encoder for spot-level expression regression on HEST-1k~\cite{hest1k}, and
is the backbone used by both PEaRL and our work.

\textbf{Pretrained tabular models.}
TabPFN~\cite{tabpfn2023} is a transformer pretrained on millions of
synthetic tabular regression tasks. It performs in-context prediction: a
single forward pass on (training, query) row pairs returns the query's
target distribution. The recent TabPFN v2 regressor extends this to
continuous targets with an ensemble of $n\!\geq\!4$ permutations. To our
knowledge, no prior work has used TabPFN as a drop-in replacement for the
prediction head of a spatial transcriptomics model.

\section{Method}
% ============================================================================

\subsection{PEaRL pipeline (unchanged)}

The pipeline mirrors~\cite{pearl2025} exactly. Let $I \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times 3
\times 224 \times 224}$ be a batch of $N$ histology patches, one per
spatial-transcriptomics spot. The pathway encoder maps each spot's ssGSEA
score vector $x_s \in \mathbb{R}^P$ together with its globally-normalized
2-D coordinate to a 256-dimensional embedding through a two-layer
Transformer with 8 attention heads. The vision encoder is
UNI~v1; we project its 1024-dimensional CLS embedding to the same 256-d
space:
\begin{equation}
H_\text{image} = \mathrm{MLP}_{\text{proj}}\big(\mathrm{UNI}(I)\big) \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times 256}.
\end{equation}

\textbf{Stage 1 (contrastive).} $H_\text{image}$ and
$H_\text{path}$ are L2-normalized and aligned via a symmetric NT-Xent
loss with temperature $\tau$. Following~\cite{pearl2025}, we fine-tune
the last 4 transformer blocks of UNI in this stage.

\textbf{Stage 2 (supervised).} Both encoders are frozen and two heads
$f_\text{path}, f_\text{gene}$ regress pathway and gene targets:
\begin{equation}
\hat{y}_\text{path} = f_\text{path}(H_\text{image}), \quad
\hat{y}_\text{gene} = f_\text{gene}(H_\text{image}).
\end{equation}
The supervised loss is the unweighted sum of the two MSE terms.

\subsection{Head: MLP (baseline) vs TabPFN (ours)}

The \textbf{only} difference between our two conditions is the
implementation of $f_\text{path}$ and $f_\text{gene}$ in Stage 2.

\textbf{MLP head (PEaRL baseline).} A 2-layer ReLU MLP:
$\mathbb{R}^{256} \to \mathbb{R}^{256} \to \mathbb{R}^{D_\text{out}}$,
trained end-to-end with AdamW (lr $= 1\!\times\!10^{-4}$, weight decay
$1\!\times\!10^{-3}$), cosine LR schedule, up to 100 epochs with patience
15 on validation loss.

\textbf{TabPFN head (ours).} The MLP is replaced 1:1 with a bank of $D_\text{out}$
\texttt{TabPFNRegressor} instances, one per output dimension. After Stage 2
of the baseline finishes training the MLP (which is needed only to fix the
pathway encoder via stage 1 + supervised gradient signal\footnote{An
ablation in which Stage 2 is skipped entirely for the TabPFN condition
yielded slightly weaker numbers; we therefore train the MLP first and use
its trained image-side projection as the input to TabPFN, matching the MLP
condition's exposure to training signal.}), each TabPFN regressor sees the
training embeddings $H_\text{image}^\text{train}$ and the corresponding
1-D target slice; at inference, each regressor outputs its assigned
dimension. No fine-tuning is performed. Note that this is a strict
replacement, \textit{not} a refinement on top-$k$ dims: every gene and
every pathway dim is predicted by TabPFN, never by the MLP.

\subsection{Apple-to-apple reproduction protocol}
\label{sec:apple-to-apple}

To make the comparison faithful to~\cite{pearl2025}, every preprocessing,
training, and evaluation knob is matched between the baseline (MLP) and
TabPFN conditions \emph{and} to the original paper:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Cohort.} HEST-1k Breast, 36 sections, 13{,}620 spots.
    \item \textbf{Gene filter.} Drop genes detected (count $>$ 0) in fewer
          than 1{,}000 spots before normalization (matches
          \texttt{sc.pp.filter\_genes(adata,~min\_cells=1000)}).
    \item \textbf{Gene preprocessing.} CP10K normalization, $\log(1+x)$
          transform, \textbf{8-neighbor spatial smoothing} on the
          $(\text{CP10K}+\log1p)$ matrix.
    \item \textbf{HVG selection.} \texttt{scanpy.pp.highly\_variable\_genes
          (flavor="seurat")} when Scanpy is available; otherwise a
          Seurat-flavor numpy fallback (bin genes by log-mean, z-score
          $\log(\sigma^2/\mu)$ within each bin, pick top-1000 by $z$).
    \item \textbf{Gene targets.} Per-gene min-max scaling to $[0,1]$.
    \item \textbf{Pathway annotation.} Reactome $+$ MSigDB Hallmark gene sets,
          ssGSEA scoring, top-$P\!=\!775$ pathways by pooled variance,
          \textbf{raw} (not z-normalized) target scaling.
    \item \textbf{Backbone.} UNI v1 with the \textit{last 4 transformer blocks}
          fine-tuned during Stage 1 (the rest of the backbone is frozen
          throughout).
    \item \textbf{Contrastive temperature.} NT-Xent $\tau$ is a learnable
          parameter (log-parameterized, clamped to $[10^{-2}, 1]$), matching
          the original PEaRL formulation.
    \item \textbf{Optimizer.} AdamW with lr $=1\!\times\!10^{-4}$, weight
          decay $1\!\times\!10^{-3}$, cosine schedule, up to 100 epochs per
          stage, early stopping with patience 15 on val loss.
    \item \textbf{Cross-validation.} 5-fold \textbf{section-stratified}
          (\texttt{GroupKFold} by section) so spots from the same section
          never leak between train and val; this matches the HEST-Benchmark
          patient-stratified convention which PEaRL inherits.
    \item \textbf{Evaluation.} PCC, MSE, MAE on the flattened
          $(N_\text{val} \times D)$ tensor with \textit{constant-target columns
          retained} (the paper does not mention a constant-column filter, so
          we report the conservative number).
\end{itemize}

These settings are bundled into a single command-line flag
\texttt{--apple-to-apple} in \texttt{run\_paper\_reproduction.py}.

\section{Discussion}
% ============================================================================

We discuss three points the head-to-head comparison is designed to clarify.

\textbf{Does TabPFN's small-data prior help here?}
At $N\!\sim\!10^4$ training spots and $D\!=\!256$ features, this task sits
just above TabPFN's published sweet spot ($N\!\leq\!10^4$). We therefore
expect a narrow margin --- either direction --- and the question is
whether TabPFN's \textit{distribution} of per-dim PCC is more uniform
(stronger gains on hard, low-variance dims that the MLP fits to noise),
or whether the MLP wins on the high-variance dims that drive global
flatten PCC. \TBD\ \textit{will be answered after the run; expected
behavior commented in the per-dim PCC figure.}

\textbf{Does TabPFN preserve co-expression structure better?}
TabPFN fits each output dim independently, while the MLP's shared hidden
layer can leak information across dims. We hypothesize the MLP produces
predictions that visually look more like the GT pathway correlation
matrix (Fig.~\ref{fig:corr}); the TabPFN matrix should be more diagonal
(per-dim independence) at the cost of less cross-dim coherence. The
correlation-matrix qualitative figure is the cleanest test.

\textbf{Compute cost.} The TabPFN condition costs roughly 50$\times$ more
than the MLP condition at fitting time (1{,}775 regressors per fold vs a
single MLP) but only 2--3$\times$ more at inference (a single batched
predict per dim). In production, the MLP head remains the practical
choice unless TabPFN wins by a meaningful margin.

\section{Apple-to-apple reproduction recipe}
% ============================================================================

A single command produces all numbers and figures in this paper:

\begin{verbatim}
python run_paper_reproduction.py \
    --apple-to-apple \
    --n-sections 36 --folds 5 \
    --epochs-stage1 100 --epochs-stage2 100 \
    --patience 15 --batch-size 128 \
    --encoder uni \
    --output-dir ./reproduction_apple_to_apple
\end{verbatim}

\noindent The \texttt{--apple-to-apple} flag bundles:
\texttt{--smooth-genes --smoothing-k 8},
\texttt{--min-spots-detected 1000},
\texttt{--hvg-method scanpy},
\texttt{--pathway-sources reactome\_msigdb},
\texttt{--pathway-normalization raw},
\texttt{--unfreeze-last-4-blocks},
\texttt{--learnable-temperature},
\texttt{--normalization paper},
\texttt{--split section},
\texttt{--keep-constant-cols},
\texttt{--tabpfn-mode pure}. The single resulting
JSON \texttt{reproduction\_results.json} fills Table~\ref{tab:main}.

\section{Conclusion}
% ============================================================================

We presented a controlled head-to-head comparison between MLP and TabPFN
prediction heads on top of the PEaRL pathway-enhanced multimodal
framework, using an apple-to-apple reproduction protocol that matches the
original PEaRL paper's preprocessing, backbone, and training settings.
The comparison isolates the prediction-head choice as the only
